Smart contract design.
Execution has a price.

My bachelor's thesis investigated whether architectural decisions—not only low-level Solidity code smells—can be connected to higher gas consumption in real Ethereum smart contracts.

RoleIndependent researcher
Dataset1,010 functions
AnalysisPython, R, regex
MethodFisher's exact test
01 / Research gap

Optimization starts before
the first line of code.

Most work on Ethereum gas efficiency focuses on implementation-level problems: expensive storage operations, poorly bounded loops, redundant computation, or unnecessary external calls. Tools such as Gasol and MadMax detect these issues through source, bytecode, static, or symbolic analysis.

The thesis examined a different layer. Two contracts can contain highly similar functions yet surround them with different roles, withdrawal mechanisms, lifecycle controls, and supporting functionality. Could those design choices help explain why nearly identical functions have different execution costs?

Research question

Which recurring smart-contract design solutions are statistically associated with high gas execution costs?

02 / Dataset

Start with code that is
almost identical.

The source dataset contained 4,965 pairs of clone functions extracted from real Ethereum smart contracts. I focused on 43 pairs with more than 95% similarity but divergent execution costs. Holding the function body nearly constant made surrounding design differences easier to inspect.

Manual comparison of those pairs generated candidate patterns. The broader validation stage then operated on 1,010 unique functions and their associated contracts.

03 / Method

Manual discovery.
Automated validation.

I grouped recurring differences into four design areas: executive-role management, withdrawal methods, market lifecycle, and additional supporting functions. This produced 18 candidate patterns.

Each pattern was translated into a tested regular expression and scanned across the full contract dataset. A Python script built contingency tables comparing pattern presence with normal or high execution cost. Fisher's exact test, run in R, evaluated statistical significance and odds ratios.

04 / Results

Six patterns survived
statistical scrutiny.

Six of the 18 candidates showed a statistically significant association with higher gas costs. The strongest observed odds ratios ranged from approximately 2.22 to 5.33.

Strongest association5.33× odds

A fund-redistribution pattern involving payable cross-contract interaction.

Owner withdrawal5.18× odds

A full-balance withdrawal guarded by ownership checks.

Static executive role4.93× odds

Hard-coded role initialization and its storage implications.

Initial-stage control4.24× odds

Administrative state transitions around a restricted market phase.

The other 12 patterns had p-values above 0.05. The study therefore did not claim that every plausible design difference increases cost: absence of sufficient evidence was kept distinct from evidence of no effect.

05 / Implications

Gas efficiency is also
an architecture concern.

The findings suggest that developers should reason about gas consumption while defining roles, lifecycle transitions, fund movement, and contract boundaries—not only during low-level Solidity optimization.

A practical continuation would turn the validated patterns into design guidelines or analysis tooling that flags potentially expensive architectural choices before deployment.

06 / Boundaries

Association, not
universal causation.

The dataset represents a specific family of real contracts, many centered on token farming, sales, and market behavior. This limits generalization to other application domains. Regex detectors can also miss semantically equivalent patterns expressed through different syntax.

Future work should use broader multi-chain datasets, semantic code representations, and machine-learning-based detectors. The reported odds ratios quantify association inside this dataset; they are not guarantees that adding one pattern will multiply every transaction's gas consumption by the same factor.
